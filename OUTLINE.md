## Module 2: Aversion and Beliefs

*Two layers of the same inquiry. Hakomi-style.*

### Part A: The Felt Sense

- Opening inquiry: "What, if any, is your relationship to AI?"
- Not a debate — a mindfulness practice
- What does the body do when you think of AI?
  Notice: contraction, tightening, opening, curiosity, dread
- "Can we be with that?" — the Hakomi posture
- Holding what comes up — fear, grief, judgment, dismissal
- These are real. We don't argue with them. We make space.

### Part B: The Story

- From the felt sense, the narrative emerges
- Surfacing and naming the beliefs:
  "AI will disconnect my children." "I'm not technical."
  "This is dehumanizing." "This isn't for people like me."
- Can we just let those be here, without needing to fix them?
- Gentle inquiry: what would it be like if that belief weren't the whole story?
- Finding more freedom — not by overriding, but by widening
- "We don't see things as they are. We see things as we are."

## Module 4: Poetic vs. Technological Attunement

*The philosophical spine. And a through-line for the whole day.*

### The Distinction

- Heidegger explained simply
- Technological attunement: everything as resource, tool, standing reserve
  — this is the dominant mode around AI, and it produces fear
- Poetic attunement: seeing the truth, beauty, and goodness in what is
  — not naive, not uncritical — open to wonder
- "What we create reflects how we are when we create it."

### The Practice — Starting Right Here

- Begin with direct experience, right now:
  the tree outside the window, the candle on the desk,
  the texture of the floor, the quality of the light
- Attune to it poetically — what is true, beautiful, good about this?
  Feel that. Don't analyze.
- From there: look at something designed — even Instagram
  What human care went into it? What does it make possible?
- From there: AI itself. What's beautiful about this?
  What human longing shaped it?

### The Through-Line

- The question for the rest of the day: can we maintain this?
- During conversations, during breakouts, during building
- We'll use Ground app throughout as an anchor — a tool for returning
- Poetic attunement is not a module. It's a practice we're in all day.

## Module 7: How to Relate Conversationally

*The Penny principle. Talk to AI like you'd talk to a human being.*

### The Metaphor

- The Penny instructions: a full dog sitter guide
  — personality, schedule, feeding, potty, health notes, tips & tricks
  — not "feed my dog twice a day and walk her"
  — full context, more than you might think is necessary
- This is how to talk to AI. For everything.
  Not just setup — for one-off tasks too.
- "Make the site more beautiful."
  vs.
  "It needs to feel warm and inviting. Things should be round, not sharp.
  Think of this color palette. It should feel like coming home."
- Same principle. Every time. Treat it like a human collaborator
  who needs real context to do real work.

### The Demo

- Watch: the bare-minimum prompt
  > "Create a website with an about page, coaching page, and bookshelf."
- Watch: Dennis's way
  > "Hey Claude — I'm a Hakomi practitioner. I care deeply about human
  > flourishing. I'm building something for people who want to slow down
  > and come back to themselves. It should feel like..."
- What's different? What's possible from each starting point?

### CLAUDE.md as Penny Instructions

- CLAUDE.md is the pre-written version of the dog sitter guide
- You write it once; Claude reads it every session
- It means you don't have to re-introduce yourself every time
- Your values, preferences, how you communicate, what matters —
  all there before a single word of code is written

### Practice

- Participants start a conversation by introducing themselves:
  > "Hey Claude — I'm a [X]. I care about [Y].
  > I've never written code before. Can we figure out together
  > what might be worth creating?"
- Opening the muscle: "I am someone who can create."

## Module 10: Basic Structure — The Landscape

*Just enough to orient. Not to master.*

- **Git** — version control
  "Saving checkpoints on your work. Like Track Changes, but for everything."
- **Database** — where data lives
  "A very organized spreadsheet your app reads from."
- **Vercel** — how things get to the world
  "One click and it's live. Someone in Tokyo can see what you just built."
- You don't need to understand these deeply to start.
  You need to know they exist, what they do, and that they're not scary.

## Module 11: Live Build

*This is what it looks like. Seed to sapling.*

- Dennis builds something real, live — something he actually wants
  OR something the cohort has asked to see made
  (NOT a pre-built demo — something that doesn't exist yet)
- Using the skills installed at the start of the day
- Pausing every 20–25 minutes — what do you notice?
  What questions arise? What's happening in you?
- The conversation is visible: how he talks to Claude,
  how he gives context, how he responds when something doesn't work
- Document the process live — participants watch the build
  AND the documentation of the build happening simultaneously
- "This is what the seed-to-sapling process looks like."
